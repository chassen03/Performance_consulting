Chapter 4 · Performance Consulting

Identify Strategic Opportunities
Reactively

A client hands you a solution. Your real job is to find the result hiding underneath it. That move is called reframing — and it turns you from an order-taker into a strategic partner.

If a problem can't be solved within the frame it was conceived, the solution lies in reframing the problem. — Brian McGreevy
SCROLL TO BEGIN THE JOURNEY ↓
01 · Why requests need reframing

Clients arrive with the answer already decided.

"Build me a training program." "Design a new comp plan." Each request arrives with a solution in mind, and the client expects you to work tactically — just deliver it.

The trouble: most performance problems have multiple causes, so they need multiple solutions. A single requested solution therefore has a high probability of low or no results. Single solutions rarely work.

Hidden assumption: problems are usually multi-causal and the client's self-diagnosis is incomplete.

Works in: complex service / knowledge orgs where performance has many moving parts. Over-engineering when: the request is genuinely single-cause or legally mandatory. Reframing is a tool, not a reflex.

02 · The engine

One mental map drives everything: SHOULD · IS · CAUSE

Every powerful question you'll learn is just a SHOULD, IS, or CAUSE question aimed at either the business row or the performance row. Learn this 2×3 grid and the rest is just how to ask.

Business (numbers)
Performance (behavior)
SHOULD
"Increase sales by 500 units"
"Reps must ask probing questions"
IS
"Sales rising at only 300 units"
"Reps are not asking them"
CAUSE
"What makes the goal hard?"
"Why aren't they asking?"

SHOULD = desired state · IS = current state · CAUSE = why the gap exists. Causes sit in three buckets: external to the org, within the org, and within individuals.

03 · Skill one

Ask powerful questions

You raise more insight by what you ask than by what you tell. A powerful question has four properties — tap each to see it in action.

Open-ended+

Can't be answered yes/no — forces a narrative.

Weak — closed"Are your reps identifying customer needs?"
Powerful"What do you need reps to do more, better, or differently in initial customer discussions?"
Cause-neutral+

Don't pre-suggest why the problem exists.

Weak — plants a cause"Do managers have sufficient data to decide?"
Powerful"Why do you think managers are challenged to make these decisions?"
Solution-neutral+

Don't assume what will fix it.

Weak — assumes training"What should supervisors do differently after this workshop?"
Powerful"What do you want supervisors to do differently on the job?"
Focused+

Aim at one cell of the Gaps Map, not the whole situation.

Weak — vague"What's going on?"
Powerful — performance IS"What actions do technicians take when the process is outside specifications?"

The strategic bonus: focused, neutral questions make it easy for the client to say "I don't know." That admission is gold — it's your opening to gather information instead of jumping to a solution.

Fails when: the culture reads questioning as stalling or insubordination, or a crisis demands action now. Mitigation: earn the right to question first through access, credibility & trust.

04 · Skill two

Sequence them in a compelling logic path

Right questions in the wrong order feel like an interrogation. The governing rule:

Start with the client's mindset — enter the map at the highest-level need they actually raised, then navigate the rest.

Path A — client entered on the performance side

"I want training for my new employees."

Performance ISWhat have you seen them doing, or not doing?
Performance SHOULDWhat do you need them to do more/better/differently?
Performance CAUSEWhy isn't that happening today?
→ Business SHOULDIf they performed that way, what's the impact on results?
Business IS → CAUSECurrent results vs. goal? What's behind the gap?

Path B — client entered on the business side

"Accidents are up; I want a refresher course and a shift competition." (Two pre-baked solutions — set them aside.)

Business SHOULDWhat are the safety goals?
Business ISHow are we tracking against each?
Business CAUSEWhy are safety results declining?
→ PerformanceOperators are key — what must they do differently to hit the goals?

Assumption: a coherent need sits under the request and the client can speak to the metrics. Fails when the request is political/symbolic, or the client is too junior to own the numbers — the path just spins.

05 · Live-conversation moves

Five core practices

1

Cash in on "I don't know"

When the client is unsure, ask: "Would it help to find that out before we move ahead?" A yes is a small commitment that quietly converts you from solution-deliverer to information-gatherer.

2

Deep-drill vague answers

"Be more collaborative" → "What specifically would they be doing to show that?" Vague words crack into "...I'm not sure" — another chance to cash in.

3

Push back on a bad solution

If the named solution won't deliver the named results, voice your concern. Silence = implicit endorsement, and you'll wear the failure later.

4

Use "Yes, and"

When they still insist, don't fight to the death. Agree to the solution and add a parallel discovery step that often nudges toward a fuller answer.

5

Be prepared to exit entirely

Every reframing talk ends in one of four outcomes:

TacticalProceed with the original solution.
Tactical + StrategicDeliver it and gather more info.
StrategicHold the solution; gather info first.
No actionDo nothing right now.

The authors note 10–20% of reframed requests end in "no action" — and call it an exciting outcome: you've saved spend on a solution that wouldn't stick, with the client's full agreement.

06 · The anchor case — watch every tool fire

Sandy & the 700 financial-services reps

The request: the ISS VP emails — "Create a career path for our reps and an educational program about it." No data. Sandy recognizes a solution request and books a one-hour reframing meeting with a blank Gaps Map.

Tap each move to see the question and the technique behind it.

1 · Open at the client's mindsetopen · solution-neutral

"What have you observed that leads you to believe a career path is needed now?"

Starts exactly where the client is — no agenda smuggled in.

2 · A business gap surfacesPath B → stay on business

Turnover jumped from <8% → 16% in six months. Exit interviews give only a vague "better opportunity." Sandy realizes she's been pulled to the business side and stays there.

3 · Widen the business impactbusiness question

"How is this attrition impacting other business results?"

Uncovers a cluster: compliance violations up, complaints up, escalations up, morale down.

4 · Cross to the performance sidefocused performance Q

"Of 68 supervisors, are any seeing very low turnover? … What are they doing differently?"

A positive-deviance angle — what's already working internally?

5 · Cash in on "I don't know"★ practice #1 — the pivot

The VP admits she can't answer. Sandy: "Would it help to find that out before we launch any solution?" VP: yes (if quick). This is the moment the role flips.

6 · Move to CAUSE & deep-drillcause-neutral + practices 1&2

"What other factors besides understaffing could be contributing?"

VP guesses understaffing (30+ open roles), but again lands on "I don't know — worth finding out."

The reframe. The career path might still happen — but alone it would never have fixed the cluster. Sandy reframed the request (build a solution → go gather the missing information) and her own role (solution provider → strategic partner). Outcome: Strategic — solution paused, discovery first.

Your transfer test: it worked because the VP owned real metrics, had an hour, trusted Sandy enough to admit "I don't know" twice, and the org had internal variation to learn from. Remove any one and the same script gets harder.

09 · 60-second check

Test yourself

Q1A client asks for "communication-skills training for managers." What's your first question and which Gaps cell does it hit?
Start at the client's mindset (performance side): "What have you observed managers doing, or not doing, that makes you think training would help?" — a performance-IS question.
Q2Fix this: "Don't you think the real issue is that managers lack time?" Which property does it break?
Breaks cause-neutral (it plants "time" as the cause) and is closed. Fix: "Why do you think managers are struggling here?"
Q3The client says "I'm not sure why retention dropped." Your exact next sentence — and which practice is it?
"Would it be helpful to find that out before we move ahead with a solution?" — Practice #1, cash in on "I don't know."
Q4The client overrules your pushback. Name the technique and write a line.
"Yes, and." e.g. "Yes, let's run the offsite — and let me interview a few people first so we design it around the real friction, and review what surfaces."
Q5Name the four end-states. Which one do ~10–20% of reframed requests reach?
Tactical · Tactical+Strategic · Strategic · No action — the last accounts for ~10–20%.
